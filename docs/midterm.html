Strategy:

Remove countries with:
- Any missing values in GDP growth. - Any missing values in FDI (% of GDP).

Imputation:
- Linear interpolation → Time-based data (inflation, trade balance). - Forward/backward fill → Data gaps within the same country. - Mean/median → Non-time-based data (e.g., unemployment). - KNN → Final refinement if gaps remain after other methods.

Handling outliers

From the pairplot, we can already see some extreme outliers:

  • FDI (% of GDP) → Some countries have very large FDI percentages (over 200%) — probably financial hubs or tax havens.
  • GDP Growth (%) → A few points showing growth rates beyond ±50% — likely data entry issues or extraordinary events (e.g., hyperinflation or post-crisis rebounds).
  • Inflation (%) → Some very high spikes — possibly reflecting hyperinflation in unstable economies.

We’ll clip values at the 1st percentile and 99th percentile to avoid extreme values distorting the model

FDI * Political stability

This SHAP interaction plot shows that FDI tends to have a stronger positive impact on GDP growth in countries with lower political stability. As FDI increases, its effect on GDP growth (measured by SHAP value) rises more sharply when political stability is low (blue) compared to when it’s high (red).

This supports the idea that FDI may be more impactful in politically fragile contexts, possibly by compensating for institutional weaknesses or lack of domestic investment.

FDI * Corruption

While FDI generally increases predicted GDP growth across all levels of corruption, this SHAP interaction plot suggests that its effect is somewhat stronger in countries with lower corruption scores. In contrast, the benefit of FDI appears slightly diminished in more corrupt environments.

However, unlike the case with political stability, this interaction is weaker and more scattered, indicating that corruption may play a lesser or more nonlinear moderating role in FDI’s impact on growth.
